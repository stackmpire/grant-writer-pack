## Theory of Change

Midland Youth Services operates from a trauma-informed, strengths-based theory of change
grounded in the social-ecological model (Bronfenbrenner, 1979) and the evidence base for
Multisystemic Therapy (MST) and Cognitive Behavioral Intervention for Trauma in Schools (CBITS).

**Core assumption:** Youth mental health outcomes are determined not solely by individual
factors but by the interaction of family, peer, school, and community systems. Interventions
that address only one system produce limited and short-lived change.

**If** youth ages 10–18 in high-poverty census tracts receive:
- Weekly individual trauma-focused CBT (12–20 sessions per youth)
- Monthly family engagement sessions (caregiver + youth together)
- School-based coordination (monthly case conference with school counselor)
- Peer-to-peer support group (bi-weekly, facilitated by trained near-peers)

**Then** we expect:
- 70% of participants will show clinically significant reduction in trauma symptom scores
  (UCLA PTSD-RI, measured at 6 and 12 months) within 12 months of enrollment
- 80% of participants will maintain or improve school attendance rates year-over-year
- 65% of caregivers will report improved family functioning (Family Assessment Device)

**By** creating durable change in the youth's closest systems (family, peer, school),
which sustains gains after program exit without requiring indefinite service intensity.

**Evidence base:** This model aligns with SAMHSA's National Registry of Evidence-Based
Programs and Practices (CBITS: Promising; TF-CBT: Well-Supported). Our FY2022–2023
outcome data (N=187) showed 73% of completers met the primary trauma symptom threshold,
exceeding the published CBITS benchmark of 65%.
